Prevent Strategy in the Workplace

A guide to understanding, recognizing, and responding to radicalization risks in professional environments to ensure safety and wellbeing for all.

Prevent is part of the UK's counter-terrorism strategy, aiming to stop people from being drawn into terrorism. In the workplace, it's about creating a safe environment where concerns can be raised and support provided.

Learn More

What is the Prevent Strategy?

Prevent is one of the four strands of the UK government's counter-terrorism strategy (CONTEST). It aims to stop people from becoming terrorists or supporting terrorism by:

  • Tackling the causes of radicalization
  • Responding to the ideological challenge of terrorism
  • Supporting vulnerable individuals through intervention
  • Working with sectors and institutions where risks exist

In the workplace, this means creating an environment where employees feel safe, respected, and able to report concerns. It's not about spying on colleagues, but about safeguarding vulnerable individuals and maintaining a secure work environment for everyone.

Key Principles
  • Safeguarding: Protecting vulnerable individuals
  • Proportionate: Reasonable, measured responses
  • Legal: Within the framework of the law
  • Preventive: Early intervention is key

Recognizing Signs of Radicalization

Radicalization can affect anyone. Being aware of potential signs helps in early identification and support. Remember that one sign alone doesn't mean someone is being radicalized, but a combination may warrant concern.

Behavioral Signs
Becoming increasingly isolated from colleagues and social circles
Expressing intolerance or hatred towards other groups or ideologies
Justifying the use of violence to achieve political, religious, or ideological goals
Showing sympathy for extremist causes or glorifying extremist figures
Significant changes in appearance or behaviour linked to ideological changes
Attempting to recruit others to an extremist ideology
Communication Signs
Using language that promotes "us vs them" narratives
Expressing a sense of grievance or injustice about national or international events
Increased secretiveness, especially around online activities
Consuming or sharing extremist material online or offline
Sudden unwillingness to discuss views or being defensive when challenged
Expressing a desire to travel to conflict zones without clear reason
Important: These signs should be considered in context. People may exhibit some of these behaviors for reasons unrelated to radicalization. The key is looking for significant, concerning changes in behavior or attitudes.

Reporting Concerns

If you're concerned that someone might be vulnerable to radicalization, it's important to act appropriately. Early intervention can help protect individuals and communities.

1
Observe & Document

Note specific behaviors, conversations, or incidents that concern you. Include dates, times, and context. Do not confront the individual directly.

2
Consult Your Designated Safeguarding Lead

Every workplace should have a designated person responsible for Prevent and safeguarding concerns. Share your observations with them.

3
Professional Assessment

The safeguarding lead will make a professional assessment. They may contact local authorities or the Prevent coordinator for guidance if needed.

Emergency & Non-Emergency Contacts
Emergency Contact

If there is an immediate threat to life or property:

Call 999
Prevent Advice

For non-emergency concerns about radicalization:

Call Police on 101

Alternatively, you can report concerns online through the government's Act Early website.

Confidentiality & Support

All concerns are treated confidentially and sensitively. The goal is always to support vulnerable individuals, not to punish them.

Confidentiality in Prevent Cases

When you report a concern through Prevent channels:

  • Your identity will be protected wherever possible
  • Information is shared only with professionals who need to know
  • The individual of concern will not be told who made the report
  • All data is handled in accordance with GDPR and data protection laws
  • The primary aim is safeguarding, not punitive action

You will not be penalized for reporting a genuine concern in good faith, even if it turns out not to be related to radicalization.
